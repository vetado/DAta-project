
========================================================================
  Section 1: Problem
========================================================================
H1: peak age differs across squat, bench, deadlift
    Predicted direction: deadlift latest, bench earliest.
H2: within each lift, peak age differs between M and F lifters.

========================================================================
  Section 2: Data source
========================================================================
Primary:        OpenPowerlifting bulk CSV (2026-05-16 snapshot).
Raw rows:       3,925,887 meet-performances x 42 columns.
Supplementary:  Wikipedia (API + scraping, see notebooks 01 & 02).

========================================================================
  Section 3: Methodology
========================================================================
Cohort filters (in order):
  1. Event == SBD
  2. Equipment != Straps
  3. Place != DQ
  4. Age in [14, 80]
  5. All three best-lift columns > 0
  6. BodyweightKg > 0

Outcome:        relative_strength = lift / BodyweightKg
Peak extraction: per (Name, Sex), per lift, age at max relative strength
H1 test:        Friedman + pairwise Wilcoxon (Bonferroni)
H2 test:        Mann-Whitney U per lift (Bonferroni, Mx excluded)

========================================================================
  Section 4: Load peak table
========================================================================
Loaded peaks_wide.csv: 441,984 lifters
Sex breakdown: {'M': np.int64(309232), 'F': np.int64(132678), 'Mx': np.int64(74)}

========================================================================
  Section 5: H1 - peak age differs across squat, bench, deadlift?
========================================================================
Friedman: chi2 = 4867, p = 0.000e+00, N = 441,984

Pairwise Wilcoxon (Bonferroni-corrected):
  Squat - Bench       median diff = +0.00 yr   p_bonf = 2.188e-04   ***
  Squat - Deadlift    median diff = +0.00 yr   p_bonf = 0.000e+00   ***
  Bench - Deadlift    median diff = +0.00 yr   p_bonf = 0.000e+00   ***

Headline:  H1 technically rejected, but the median pairwise
           differences are <= 0.5 yr and the direction is
           OPPOSITE the prediction (deadlift earliest, not latest).
           In practice: peak age is the same across all three lifts.
Figure:    reports/figures/peak_age_by_lift.png

========================================================================
  Section 6: H2 - within each lift, peak age differs M vs F?
========================================================================
Per lift, Mann-Whitney U (Bonferroni-corrected):
  Squat       med M = 23.5   med F = 26.0   diff (M-F) = -2.50 yr   p_bonf = 0.000e+00   ***
  Bench       med M = 23.5   med F = 26.0   diff (M-F) = -2.50 yr   p_bonf = 0.000e+00   ***
  Deadlift    med M = 23.5   med F = 26.0   diff (M-F) = -2.50 yr   p_bonf = 0.000e+00   ***

Headline:  Women peak 2.5 years LATER than men in all three lifts.
           The effect is identical across lifts (rules out a
           lift-specific physiological story). Cause may be
           physiology OR sampling era (women's powerlifting expanded
           in the 2010s-2020s; women in OPL are on average newer to
           the sport). This study cannot distinguish them.
Figure:    reports/figures/peak_age_by_lift_and_sex.png

========================================================================
  Section 8: Limitations
========================================================================
1. Single-meet lifters dominate the marginals: most lifters have
   only one cohort meet, so their three peak ages collapse to a
   single value. This is why H1's median differences come out at
   <= 0.5 yr (a 'practical null') despite Friedman's p approx 0.

2. Sex effect could be sampling era, not physiology: women's
   competitive powerlifting expanded mainly in the 2010s-2020s.
   Women in OPL are on average newer to the sport, so 'peak in
   observed data' is biased toward later ages.

3. Age missingness is large (37% in raw, 43.6% effective). OPL
   records lifts more reliably than ages. We have no way to test
   whether unrecorded-age lifters peak differently.

4. Dedup tie-breaking is by input order (first meet wins among
   ties for max relative strength). Alternative rules would give
   slightly different per-lifter peak ages but no material change.

5. Equipment is not adjusted for in the H2 test. If M and F use
   different equipment distributions, part of the M-F difference
   could be an Equipment difference. The natural follow-up is per-
   lift OLS regression peak_age ~ Sex + Equipment.

========================================================================
  Section 9: Conclusion
========================================================================
In OpenPowerlifting's competitive data, peak relative-strength age
does not meaningfully differ across the squat, bench, and deadlift
(the three medians sit within 0.5 yr of each other), but it differs
substantively between sexes: women peak about 2.5 years later than
men, identically across all three lifts. The sex effect is the
project's headline finding; whether it reflects physiology or OPL's
sampling era is an open question this analysis cannot resolve.
